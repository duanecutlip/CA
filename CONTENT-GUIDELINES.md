## Language Rules — Banned or Restricted Phrases

### Never Use
- **"Lock in prices"** or any variation — technically inaccurate and potentially misleading. The correct statement is: *"The funeral home accepts your preneed plan as payment in full for the covered services."*
- **"Affordable"** as a primary descriptor for preneed or funeral services — frames cost minimization as the goal, which is the wrong message for this brand
- **"Simple"** as a plan tier or package category — this word means something different to every person. Do not use it to describe a level of service.
- **"The Carolinas"** — SC is not in scope (see Compliance section above)
- **"Dual-state licensed"** or **"NC and SC licensed"** — not currently accurate

### Use With Care
- **"Inflation Proof Guarantee"** — preferred replacement for "lock in prices" language. Accurate and compelling.
- **"Cash advance items"** (death certificates, obituaries, flowers, etc.) — always clarify these are **not** covered by the preneed plan and will vary at time of need
- Any specific dollar figures for competitor pricing — verify before publishing; costs change

## Cost Presentation Rules

These rules exist because presenting funeral costs incorrectly can steer people toward decisions they'll regret, or set false expectations before they've had a real conversation.

### Never Do
- **Lead with the floor price.** Showing "$1,995 direct cremation" first anchors every other option as an expensive premium. Visitors will mentally subtract from that number, not build up from it.
- **Use comparison tables** (e.g., Simple / Standard / Full tiers) — they train visitors to select the cheapest column as the "rational" choice
- **Steer visitors toward cremation over traditional burial** — or vice versa. The right choice depends on the family; the website shouldn't answer that question for them.
- **Answer questions the visitor didn't bring.** If they came wondering generally about planning, don't push them into a specific plan type before they've talked to Duane.

### Always Do
- **Lead with context and purpose** before mentioning cost — why funerals matter, what they provide for the family left behind
- **Present ranges, not floors** — e.g., "most families in NC spend between $X and $X depending on what serves them best"
- **Note what the NFDA median excludes.** The commonly cited $8,300 national median does not include cemetery, burial vault, grave marker, or opening/closing fees — which often add $5,000–$10,000 or more. Pointing this out builds credibility.
- **Emphasize value, not price.** The families who struggle most afterward are rarely the ones who spent more than they planned — they're the ones who left something out.

### The Core Value Argument (use in cost-related copy)
Cutting a service to save $500 won't change anyone's financial picture for a week. But a service that falls short of what a family needed stays with people for years. Meaningful rituals — gathering, remembrance, community — are how human beings heal from loss. They are not extras.

### Cost Reference Ranges (NC, current as of early 2026)
- Direct cremation (transport + cremation, no service): $1,995–$3,900
- Cremation with meaningful services: $4,000–$12,000
- Traditional burial with services: $10,000–$20,000 (before cemetery costs)
- Cemetery costs (plot, vault, opening/closing, marker): often $5,000–$10,000+ additional
- Funeral costs have risen approximately 40% over the last decade

## Identity & Tone Rules

### Faith
- Duane is faith-driven and that is part of his story — but keep faith references **general**
- Do not specify Baptist or any denomination — too narrow for the broad audience and could alienate people of other Christian traditions or faith backgrounds
- Acceptable: "active in his church," "faith is the foundation of this work," "faith-driven"

### Politics
- **No political references anywhere on the site** — not implied, not inferred

### Tone
- Duane is a **listener, not a salesman**. Copy should reflect that he asks the right questions and helps families find what genuinely serves them — not that he presents packages or closes deals.
- **Warm, authoritative, and honest.** Not corporate. Not aggressive. Not fear-based.
- When discussing difficult topics (cost, death, grief), be direct and compassionate — not clinical, not evasive.

### Ham Radio / ARRL
- Frame as **community service and preparation mindset** — not as a hobbyist interest
- Connects to themes of: service, patience, technical capability, preparedness

## Design Philosophy (Don't Change Without Discussion)

These design decisions were made deliberately for the primary audience (women 60–72):

- **Large text**: 20px desktop, 18px mobile — mandatory for the age group
- **No animations, no auto-carousels, no dark mode** — seniors find these disorienting
- **No hover dropdowns in navigation** — seniors struggle with hover menus; every nav item clicks through
- **Phone number everywhere** — appears multiple times on every page because this audience calls, they don't fill out forms
- **Print stylesheets** — seniors print web pages; blog posts and guides must render cleanly when printed
- **Warm, light color palette** — calming and trustworthy, opposite of aggressive insurance sites
