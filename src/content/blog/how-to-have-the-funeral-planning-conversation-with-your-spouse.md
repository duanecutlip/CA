It's one of the conversations nobody wants to have.

Not because you don't care about your spouse. Not because you're morbid or obsessed with death. It's because the conversation feels like you're inviting something in — like talking about it makes it more real, or more imminent.

But here's what Duane Cutlip hears from families every week, after twenty years of preneed planning in North Carolina:

*"I wish we had talked about this sooner."*

Almost never: *"I wish we hadn't."*

---

## Why Most Couples Never Have This Conversation

The reasons are understandable:

- It feels like you're predicting something. (You're not — you're preventing something worse.)
- It's uncomfortable to picture life without the other person.
- There's always a "better time" to bring it up. (There usually isn't.)
- It feels like a topic for old people. (It's actually a topic for healthy people.)

The couples who plan ahead aren't the ones who have a special relationship with death. They're the ones who love each other enough to handle the hard conversations.

---

## What Happens When You Don't Talk About It

When one spouse passes without a plan — or without the other spouse knowing what they wanted — the surviving partner is left to make $15,000 decisions in the first 48 hours after loss.

Not just financial decisions. Decisions like:

- **Burial or cremation?** You may know what you *think* your spouse wanted. But did they ever say it out loud? Did they write it down?
- **Where to be buried?** Near family? In a specific cemetery? Cremated remains scattered somewhere meaningful?
- **What kind of service?** A traditional church funeral? A celebration of life? Something small and private?
- **Music, readings, who speaks?** All of this gets decided — quickly, under grief.

Disagreements between adult children about what their parent would have wanted are among the most painful family conflicts Duane sees. A documented plan eliminates that conflict completely.

## How to Actually Start the Conversation

You don't have to have a long, serious, sit-down-at-the-kitchen-table conversation. Sometimes the easiest way in is through something smaller.

**Use a moment that's already in the air.**
A friend's loss. A news story. A health scare. Someone at church whose family was caught off guard. When those things happen, they naturally open a door: *"That made me think — I don't think we've ever really talked about what we'd each want."*

**Lead with your own wishes, not theirs.**
It's easier for most people to talk about themselves than to feel like they're being interrogated. Start with *"I've been thinking about what I'd want"* rather than *"What would you want?"* It takes the pressure off and usually leads to a genuine exchange.

**Frame it around love, not logistics.**
The most natural way to frame this conversation is: *"I love you, and I don't want to leave things unsettled for you. Can we figure this out together?"* That's not morbid. That's care.

**Keep it light the first time.**
You don't have to decide everything in one conversation. The goal of the first conversation is just to start — to establish that this is something you're willing to talk about. The details can come later.

---

## What to Actually Decide Together

Once you've opened the door, here are the things worth getting on paper:

- Burial, cremation, or something else?
- Where (which funeral home, which cemetery, or where to scatter ashes)?
- What kind of service — church, funeral home, graveside, celebration of life, or something informal?
- Music, readings, specific people you'd want involved?
- Anything you specifically *don't* want?

You don't need to have an opinion on every detail. But the basic framework — burial vs. cremation, roughly what kind of service — goes a long way toward protecting your spouse from having to guess.
